What is Schema Markup? A Beginner's Guide to Structured Data

Schema markup is a form of microdata that you add to your website's HTML to help search engines understand the content on your pages. By using structured data vocabulary from Schema.org, you can provide explicit clues about the meaning of your content.

When you add schema markup to your web pages, you're essentially speaking the language of search engines. This structured data helps Google, Bing, and other search engines display your content in rich results—also known as rich snippets—which can include star ratings, prices, images, and more.

Why Schema Markup Matters

Search engines have become incredibly sophisticated, but they still need help understanding context. For example, if you mention "Apple" on your page, is it the fruit or the technology company? Schema markup clarifies this ambiguity.

Schema.org was founded in 2011 by Google, Microsoft, Yahoo, and Yandex to create a shared vocabulary for structured data. Today, over 800 different schema types are available for everything from recipes to events to products.

How Schema Markup Works

Schema markup uses a specific vocabulary of tags that you add to your HTML. These tags tell search engines what each piece of content represents—whether it's a person, an organization, a product, a review, or any other entity. The data is typically added using JSON-LD, Microdata, or RDFa formats.

JSON-LD is Google's recommended format because it's easy to implement and maintain. It works by embedding a script block in your page's head or body section containing all your structured data in a structured JSON format.

Common Schema Types

There are schema types for virtually every kind of content. Article schema helps news articles and blog posts appear in Google News and Top Stories carousels. Product schema enables price and availability display in search results. LocalBusiness schema helps local companies appear in the Local Pack.

Event schema allows your events to appear directly in Google Search with dates and locations. Recipe schema enables rich results with cooking time, calories, and user ratings. Review schema lets you display star ratings in search results.

Using the right schema type for your content is crucial for maximizing your visibility in search results.
